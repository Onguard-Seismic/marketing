North America Marketing Plan 2026
The Situation

Onguard has been in the US market for about five years. In that time, we've built a track record, real project wins, and a handful of strong client relationships. What we haven't built is a systematic way to generate leads.

Right now, the top of our funnel runs on referrals, word of mouth, and organic search. Those aren't bad channels — but they're passive. They depend on someone else remembering to mention us, or a prospect finding us mid-crisis when they've already exhausted other options. That's not a growth strategy. It's a survival strategy.

This plan changes that. The goal is a small, sustainable, in-house marketing effort that generates a consistent flow of qualified leads — enough to support the $1M revenue target for 2026 and build the foundation for what comes after.

Our Products

Two anchor product lines cover the full range of US tank configurations.

OG Pro

For tanks with flat bottoms on concrete pads or on skirts. Anchors attach around the circumference of the tank base. This is the core product — the design that started Onguard, proven in real seismic events, and the one that scales to the largest industrial storage tanks. High-value projects, repeatable design, strong track record. The workhorse.

GC Pro

For shop-built tanks with integrated legs or stands. The GC Pro replaces the standard adjustable foot pad with an engineered seismic anchor incorporating three elements: a height-adjustable thread (same installation as a standard foot pad), an articulating ball base that sits flat on sloped concrete rather than transmitting moment loads into the tank leg, and a tuned yielding fuse that absorbs seismic energy in controlled tension and compression and is replaceable after an event.

The engineering unlock: The GC Pro is classified as a Buckling-Restrained Brace (BRB) under ASCE 7-22. That classification allows response modification factors up to R = 8, reducing seismic design forces by more than 60%. Thinner slabs, lighter tank stand steel, less rebar, shallower foundations. For retrofit projects where an existing slab can't be torn out, the GC Pro is often what makes the design viable — it unclogs projects that were otherwise stuck.

Primary target audience for the GC Pro is tank builders — the goal is for fabricators to adopt it as their standard foot pad on any shop-built tank. If one or two larger tank companies standardize on GC Pro, the volume impact is significant without requiring a project-by-project engineering sale.

Engineering Toolkit

Both products are supported by calculation spreadsheets that determine the correct anchor model, forces, and full design specifications. The goal is for engineering firms and tank builders to run the numbers and spec Onguard anchors without handing the design work back to us. This is also what engineering firms have asked for: the ability to reference Onguard anchors as catalog hardware with known values, the same way they'd spec any other component.

Who We're Talking To
Primary: The Specifying Engineer

Structural or civil engineer at a firm that encounters tank anchorage work a handful of times a year. Mid-career, 40s–50s. Has a process and isn't looking to reinvent it — until they're stuck. The moment Onguard is most useful to them is when their design produces results that don't work in the field: slab specs no one can build, anchor bolt patterns that don't fit, cost estimates that kill the project. Our job is to be findable — and credible — when that moment arrives.

Secondary: The End-User / Risk Owner

Facility owner, VP of Operations, CFO, or Director of Engineering at a winery, distillery, water utility, or industrial facility. They own the asset. They own the risk. Most haven't thought hard about seismic exposure because nothing has prompted them to. The goal here is to get upstream of the specifying engineer — to the person who pushes Onguard into the conversation when a project starts.

Emerging: The API / Large Industrial Market

Petroleum terminals, large chemical storage, municipal water infrastructure. Older industry, higher skepticism, higher bar for credibility. This is a long-term play — but the revenue potential per project is an order of magnitude larger than beverage-sector work. One 2M-gallon tank system can nearly make a year.

Strategy
Core Positioning

Performance and resilience-focused seismic design, informed by observed earthquake behavior. That's the lane. It's credible, it's defensible, and it invites engineers into the conversation rather than alienating them.

What we're not doing: positioning Onguard as "API is wrong." That message creates friction with the very people we need as allies. The better frame: codes have objectives and limitations, and real-world seismic events keep teaching us better ways to achieve them. Onguard is part of that conversation — not a critic of it.

US Market Messaging

The brand guidelines were written for a New Zealand audience where tank failures from seismic events are culturally recent and well understood. In the US, dramatic damage messaging lands as confusing rather than relevant. Most US facility owners haven't seen a tank fail. The message that actually reaches them is different: asset protection, business continuity, and design efficiency.

"Onguard makes seismic code compliance buildable" and "protect the operation before the event requires it" are the angles that open conversations here. The engineering audience responds to a third message: the OG Pro and GC Pro calculation tools reduce design iteration and produce results that are actually constructible.

Content Progression

Authority builds over time through a phased approach — not with a single post:

  • Phase 1 — Pure education: Seismic behavior, tank dynamics, real-world event lessons. Build credibility before connecting it to Onguard.
  • Phase 2 — Thought leadership + soft brand association: Engineering observations connected to Onguard's methodology and field experience. (Current phase.)
  • Phase 3 — Methodology leadership: The MBA method, post-event analysis, how ductile design changes what's buildable.
  • Phase 4 — Subtle commercial pull: Case studies, resilience outcomes, insurance and operational continuity implications.
Channels and Tactics
Channel 1
LinkedIn — Primary Channel

LinkedIn is where our target audience — engineers, facility managers, project owners — actually is. Early results confirm this. It's the highest-leverage channel at this budget.

Tactics
  • Posting cadence: 2× per week, minimum. Technical education, real-world observations, project insights. Jared drafts and reviews; Claude Code handles first drafts and scheduling prep.
  • Targeted direct messaging: A defined outreach sequence for engineers and facility managers at high-value accounts. Researched, personalized, professional — not a cold pitch.
  • Community engagement: Commenting on relevant posts, responding to technical questions, building relationships before we need anything from them. This is where reputation compounds quietly.
Channel 2
Blog / Website Content

The website currently shows that Onguard exists. It needs to do more than that. A focused set of well-written, SEO-relevant articles can bring in engineers who are searching for exactly the problem we solve.

  • Cadence: 1 article per month, targeted to terms engineers actually search when they're stuck (e.g., "tank anchorage seismic design," "aboveground storage tank seismic requirements").
  • Content gets repurposed from LinkedIn posts and case studies — not starting from scratch every time.
Channel 3
Case Study Development

Case studies answer the question every potential client eventually asks: "Has someone like me already used this?" We develop 2–3 new case studies from recent projects — winery, water utility, industrial — formatted for both web and PDF. These feed LinkedIn posts, DM outreach, and email follow-ups.

Channel 4
Speaking Opportunities / Trade Shows

NISTM and API-adjacent events are where we establish credibility in front of the right rooms. The lesson from NISTM: ask earlier, ask specifically, and have a prepared topic ready.

  • Submit for at least one speaking slot at NISTM 2026 or 2027
  • Attend relevant trade shows with a clear plan: who we're targeting, what we want to leave with
  • Follow up within 48 hours of every show contact — emails drafted before the show, personalized after
Channel 5
Engineering Toolkit Distribution

The OG Pro and GC Pro spec sheets and calculation spreadsheets are marketing assets, not just technical documents. When an engineering firm has them on file, they can spec Onguard anchors without calling us — which is exactly what most structural engineers prefer. They want to own the design. These tools let them.

  • Finalize OG Pro and GC Pro spec sheets in catalog format
  • Distribute to target engineering firms as a direct outreach offer: "We put together a spec reference if it's useful for future projects"
  • Include as a gated download on the website (email capture)
  • Use as a LinkedIn touchpoint and trade show follow-up vehicle

This channel costs nothing to run once the documents are finalized. It is the closest thing we have to scalable pull marketing — the tool does the outreach work after it's distributed.

Channel 6
Video Content — Animated Mini-Series

A 6-episode animated series: The Adventures of Ductile Dan & OG. South Park-style character animation — professional enough for LinkedIn and trade show screens, approachable enough to hold a non-engineer's attention. Each episode runs 60–90 seconds and uses a fixed production framework (mobilization, arrival at site, resolution) surrounding one variable central scene where the engineering lesson is sketched out on a whiteboard or chalkboard. Only the technical lesson scene changes per episode — the surrounding framework is built once in the pilot and reused, which drives down per-episode cost significantly.

  • Characters: Ductile Dan (English-NZ hybrid, calm seismic engineer), OG (Kiwi field sidekick), Seamus (OG's German Shepherd — the early-warning system)
  • Episode topics: Capacity-based design / Energy dissipation vs. absorption / Anchor ductility vs. slab stiffness / Traditional anchor failures / API 650 vs. ASCE 7 vs. Onguard / Common tank design errors
  • Output per episode: Horizontal MP4 (LinkedIn/YouTube) + vertical crop (Instagram Reels), optional captions
  • Onguard supplies: Scripts and Scene 4 educational sketches for each episode

The honest constraint: Jared runs North American sales, current project management, and this marketing effort simultaneously. The marketing only works if the workflows are built to minimize per-piece execution time.

The goal is batch production — 3–4 weeks of LinkedIn posts drafted in one session, DM sequences templated for quick personalization, case studies built from existing project notes. Claude Code makes this realistic. Without a production workflow, the marketing slips when projects get busy. That pattern needs to be broken structurally, not by willpower.
